01 What this system is ▶

The UPAMM Dashboard is the single system of record for representation work: member intake, staffing/safety concerns, grievances and workload reviews, contractual deadlines, obligations owed in both directions, and meeting notes. Everything a steward opens, tracks, or resolves lives in the dashboard so the whole steward team works from the same picture and nothing falls through the cracks.

02 Getting into the dashboard ▶

The dashboard is at upamm-dashboard.pages.dev. Sign in with your @upamm.org Google account.

Need access? Contact the Chief Steward to be added. If a browser gives you a login loop, use Microsoft Edge or Chrome (some Safari privacy settings block sign-in).

Bookmark it on your phone — most intake happens in the moment, on the unit.

03 A member comes to you with a problem ▶

This is the front door. However the contact comes in — form, email, group message, or a hallway conversation:

  1. Assign the next case number. Assign P##-YYYY — LastName (Pending) at first contact.
  2. Create the case folder in Pending with its standard subfolders.
  3. Log the intake in the dashboard — record the source and the initial issue.
  4. Decide the track and move the case: needs a formal filing → G## Grievance; resolved informally → I## Informal; MERC/PERA route → U##; no further action → close as P##.
The number is assigned once and never changes. When the track is decided, the folder is renamed and moved — but it keeps its number.
04 A member is called into a discipline meeting URGENT ▶

If a member is called into a meeting that could lead to discipline, this is time-sensitive — act right away.

  1. Weingarten first. The member has the right to union representation in a meeting they reasonably believe could lead to discipline — but they must ask for it. Tell members: if called in, request a steward before answering questions.
  2. Get there, or send a steward. Don't let the member go in alone if it can be avoided.
  3. Your role in the room: you may consult with the member, ask for clarification of questions, and take notes. You are not a silent witness.
  4. Log it immediately after — create or update the case, record who was present, what was asked, and any commitments made.
Not sure if it's "just a conversation"? Treat it as if it could lead to discipline, get representation, and contact the Chief Steward right away. See Your rights & the member's for Weingarten, Garrity, and Loudermill.
05 Raising a staffing / safety concern ▶
  1. Capture it — member submits a Quick/Full concern form, or you enter it from a verbal report.
  2. Create the concern case in the Staffing/Safety Concern Tracker; set the concern type (staffing ratio, unsafe assignment, equipment, coverage gap, other).
  3. Notify management / labor relations. Generate the notification from the dashboard; the goal is a written acknowledgement of receipt so the concern is on the record.
  4. Track acknowledgement. Unacknowledged cases are flagged. Mark Acknowledged when you receive confirmation.
  5. Aggregate repeat reports. If several members report the same concern, link their submissions to the one case so the count reflects how many people it affects.
  6. Escalate if needed. If it isn't acknowledged or resolved, escalate to a formal grievance — the dashboard creates the linked grievance for you.
The notification you generate for leadership summarizes unit, concern type, submission count, reporting period, and the issue — without member names. Names stay internal.
06 Filing a formal grievance ▶
  1. Confirm a contractual basis — identify the CBA article(s) violated. Grievances follow the Article 16 procedure.
  2. Mind the timeline. Grievances are time-limited. Use the dashboard's Article 16 deadline calculator to set each step's due date — don't estimate from memory.
  3. Open the case — rename the folder to G##-YYYY — [type] — LastName, move it to Grievances, create the grievance record.
  4. Log every step and communication in the case event log so deadlines and obligations both directions stay current.
A missed deadline can cost a grievance. When you file, immediately set the next step's deadline in the dashboard.
07 Logging a workload review ▶
  1. Intake via the WLR form (bit.ly/upamm-wlr) or verbal entry.
  2. Open on the WLR track — G##-YYYY — WLR — LastName.
  3. Track timelines per the CBA and log progress and any management response in the case.
  4. If it escalates to a formal grievance, the folder keeps its number and drops the WLR tag.
08 Tracking a contractual deadline ▶
  1. Enter the deadline as a deadline, not just a note — so it shows up in deadline scans and the health banner.
  2. Link it to the case by grievance number so it appears on the case file.
  3. Give it a real due date from the CBA / Article 16 calculator, not a guess.
Deadline hygiene: every contractual clock lives in the dashboard. If it's only in your head or your inbox, it will be missed.
09 Logging a meeting ▶
  1. Notes — meeting notes are captured and saved to the shared Drive.
  2. Log the meeting in the dashboard — type, date, and grievance number if it's case-related.
  3. Pull out action items — anything management committed to goes to "Mgmt Owes Us"; anything the union committed to becomes a task.
10 Send the form, or fill it yourself? ▶

Every member issue can start with a form. The question is whether the member fills it out or you do.

Send the form to the member when… they can describe the issue clearly themselves, it isn't time-critical, and you want their own words on the record (helpful later for a grievance or concern).
Fill it out yourself (verbal intake) when… the member is upset or overwhelmed, it's urgent, it came up in a hallway conversation, or they'd rather talk than type. Capture it in the dashboard while it's fresh, then confirm the details back to them.
11 What counts as a staffing / safety concern ▶

Any situation where staffing, assignment, coverage, or resources put safe patient care — or a PA's ability to practice safely — at risk. Common examples:

  • Staffing ratio — too few PAs/providers for the patient load or acuity.
  • Unsafe assignment — beyond a PA's competency, scope, or credentialed privileges, or without adequate supervision/support.
  • Coverage gap — no coverage for breaks, call, cross-coverage, or a departed colleague's panel.
  • Equipment / resources — missing, broken, or inadequate equipment or systems needed to deliver safe care.
  • Pattern of understaffing — the same shortfall recurring over time, even if any single day seems manageable.
A concern doesn't have to become a grievance. Many resolve by documenting the issue, requesting acknowledgement, and tracking whether it recurs. Log it either way — patterns are what build the case.

13 Your rights & the member's ▶

Plain-language summaries for steward use — not legal advice. Michigan Medicine is a public employer (University of Michigan), which is why the public-employee protections below apply.

Weingarten

NLRB v. J. Weingarten, Inc. (1975)

Right to union representation during an investigatory interview the member reasonably believes could lead to discipline. The employer doesn't have to offer it.

Do: tell the member — ask for a steward before answering questions.

Garrity

Garrity v. New Jersey (1967)

A public employee can't be forced to choose between self-incrimination and their job. Statements compelled under threat of discipline generally can't be used against them criminally.

Do: if a matter could be criminal, stop and call the Chief Steward before the member answers.

Loudermill

Cleveland Bd. of Ed. v. Loudermill (1985)

Public employees with a property interest in their job are entitled to due process before termination — notice of the charges and a chance to respond.

Do: if termination is on the table, confirm the member got notice and a real chance to respond.

Veterans' Preference

Michigan PA 205 of 1897 · MCL 35.401 et seq.

Honorably discharged veterans in Michigan public employment can't be removed or suspended except for official misconduct (with a right to a hearing), and get seniority preference if positions are reduced.

Do: if the member is a veteran facing discipline or layoff, flag it to the Chief Steward — extra protections may apply.

These are starting points. For any specific situation, confirm details with the Chief Steward before advising a member.
14 HIPAA hard stops ▶
No patient data — anywhere in the union system. No patient names, MRNs, DOBs, dates of service, diagnoses, or screenshots of the medical record in the dashboard, in Drive, in forms, in email, or in meeting notes.

Describe clinical situations generically: "a high-acuity patient," "a full panel," "an unstable admission" — never identifiable detail.

If clinical documentation is genuinely needed as evidence (rare), don't collect or store it yourself. Stop and contact the Chief Steward for the proper, compliant channel. Protecting members means protecting patients' privacy too.

15 Data hygiene — dashboard vs. Drive ▶

Two homes, clear roles:

  • Dashboard — the structured record: cases, statuses, deadlines, event logs, obligations, concern tracking, tasks, meeting entries. Where you work a case.
  • Drive — the document store: the actual files. Each case folder has standard subfolders (Submitted to Management, Received from Management, In-Process Drafts, Supporting Documents, Emails & Correspondence, Meeting Notes).
Rule of thumb: facts and timelines go in the dashboard; files go in Drive. Link the Drive documents from the case so both stay connected. And remember §14 — nothing with patient data goes in either.
16 Common mistakes ▶
  1. Letting a member walk into an investigatory meeting alone — Weingarten only works if representation is requested.
  2. Tracking a contractual deadline in your head or inbox instead of entering it in the dashboard.
  3. Reusing or skipping a case number — numbers are assigned once and never reused.
  4. Putting patient-identifiable information anywhere in the union system.
  5. Logging an issue as a note but never setting its concern type, track, or deadline — so it never surfaces in scans.
  6. Including member names in a notification meant for leadership — keep names internal.
  7. Resolving something verbally and never recording it, so the pattern is invisible next time.
