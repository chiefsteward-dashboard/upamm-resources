ADA Americans with Disabilities Act — Workplace Accommodations ▶

What it covers

The ADA requires Michigan Medicine to provide reasonable accommodations to employees with a qualifying disability — meaning a physical or mental condition that substantially limits a major life activity. This includes chronic conditions, mental health diagnoses, recovery from injury or surgery, and more.

What "reasonable accommodation" means

  • Modified schedule or shift
  • Modified duties (temporary or permanent)
  • Remote work or hybrid arrangement
  • Equipment or assistive technology
  • A different workspace or reduced noise environment
  • Leave as an accommodation (beyond FMLA)
  • Reassignment to a vacant position as a last resort
You don't have to say "I need an ADA accommodation." You only have to communicate that you have a condition and need a change at work. HR is responsible for recognizing when the ADA applies.

The interactive process

When you request an accommodation, Michigan Medicine must engage in a good-faith interactive process — a dialogue about what you need and what's possible. They must consider your request seriously. "We can't do that" is not an automatic answer.

  • You have the right to have a union representative present at any meeting about your accommodation
  • Michigan Medicine can ask for documentation from your provider — but cannot demand your full medical records
  • The provider note should describe your functional limitations, not your diagnosis
  • Michigan Medicine must respond in a reasonable timeframe — indefinite delay is not acceptable
Watch for: Being offered an "accommodation" that doesn't actually address your limitation, or being told the process requires you to accept whatever is offered. The interactive process is a negotiation, not a take-it-or-leave-it.

At Michigan Medicine

HR Leave Specialist (HRSC) owns the ADA interactive process. Contact: 734-647-5538, option #4. Fax documentation to: 734-936-9526.

FMLA Family and Medical Leave Act — Job-Protected Leave ▶

What it covers

FMLA provides up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per year for qualifying reasons, including your own serious health condition or care for a qualifying family member.

Eligibility requirements

  • Worked at U of M for at least 12 months
  • Worked at least 1,250 hours in the past 12 months
  • Your condition qualifies as a "serious health condition" (most do)

Key rights under FMLA

  • Your job — or an equivalent job — is protected while you're on leave
  • Your health benefits continue during leave
  • You can take leave intermittently (individual days, half-days, reduced schedule)
  • You cannot be disciplined or retaliated against for taking FMLA leave
  • FMLA leave can run concurrently with CBA sick/medical leave — ask HR about this upfront
Intermittent FMLA is a real option. If your condition causes unpredictable flares, bad days, or medical appointments, you can apply for intermittent FMLA rather than taking a full block of leave. This protects those absences retroactively.

What FMLA does NOT cover

  • Paid leave — FMLA is unpaid (though CBA provisions may provide pay)
  • Conditions that don't qualify as "serious" under federal definition
  • Leave beyond 12 weeks (ADA may fill this gap)

PWDCRA Michigan Persons with Disabilities Civil Rights Act — State Protection ▶

What it is

Michigan's PWDCRA is the state-level companion to the ADA. It prohibits disability discrimination and requires reasonable accommodation — and in some ways it's broader than federal law.

Where PWDCRA is broader than ADA

  • Applies to employers with 1 or more employees (ADA requires 15+)
  • Broader definition of "disability" — does not require the condition to be substantially limiting in the same way
  • Covers conditions that are episodic or in remission
  • Includes protection against discrimination based on use of protected leave
Practical implication: If a federal ADA claim is uncertain (e.g., condition is borderline or intermittent), a PWDCRA claim may still be strong. UPAMM can help assess this.

At Michigan Medicine

PWDCRA claims are filed with the Michigan Department of Civil Rights (MDCR). In practice, the same HR process applies — but if HR denies an accommodation and you believe PWDCRA applies, that's a union matter. Contact UPAMM.

CBA UPAMM Contract (CBA) — Negotiated Leave & Protections ▶

What the CBA adds

Your collective bargaining agreement with Michigan Medicine provides leave and related rights that are in addition to — not instead of — federal and state law. CBA provisions often provide paid benefits where federal law only provides unpaid protection.

Key CBA provisions to be aware of

  • Medical leave of absence — terms and duration per contract
  • Sick leave accrual and use — may run concurrently with FMLA
  • Short-term disability — if applicable under your benefit plan
  • Job protection during leave — CBA may provide additional protection beyond FMLA
  • Return-to-work rights — contract language on restoration after leave
Always ask HR: "Are my CBA leave provisions running concurrently with FMLA?" If they are, your paid leave covers the same period as FMLA — so you're not burning FMLA time separately. If HR doesn't address this, contact UPAMM.

What UPAMM can help with

If Michigan Medicine is not applying CBA leave provisions correctly, or is treating a leave situation differently than the contract requires, that may be a grievable issue. Contact UPAMM before you accept any leave arrangement that doesn't feel right.

⚠ Interactive Process The ADA Interactive Process — What It Really Means ▶

What it is

When you notify Michigan Medicine that you have a condition and need a change at work, HR is required by law to engage in an interactive process — a good-faith, back-and-forth discussion to identify an effective accommodation.

What you'll typically receive

An email or letter from an HR Leave Specialist asking for a provider note with:

  • The nature of your condition
  • How it affects your ability to perform essential job functions
  • What restrictions exist
  • What accommodation is recommended
  • Expected duration
Before you submit that provider note: Contact UPAMM. You have the right to understand what you're submitting, what Michigan Medicine will do with it, and what happens next. You also have the right to union representation in any follow-up meeting.

Your rights during the interactive process

  • Michigan Medicine must respond — they cannot ignore your request
  • They can request documentation, but cannot demand your full medical history
  • They must consider your requested accommodation — but can propose alternatives
  • If they deny your request, they must explain why and what alternatives were considered
  • You can propose your own accommodation — you don't have to accept whatever is offered
  • You have the right to union representation at any meeting about your accommodation

What Work Connections does

Work Connections is the U of M office that facilitates return-to-work planning for employees coming off medical leave. They work with your provider's restrictions to determine what modified duty or accommodation options exist. They are separate from HR but frequently involved in the same cases. If you're on leave and preparing to return, you'll likely hear from both.

Keep records of everything. Date every call, email, and meeting. Note who you spoke with and what was said. If something is agreed to verbally, follow up in writing: "As we discussed today, I understand that..." This documentation matters.

Employer Obligations — ADA Interactive Process

ADA / PWDCRA

What Michigan Medicine must do

  • Engage in a good-faith interactive process — they cannot simply deny without discussion
  • Consider the member's requested accommodation before proposing alternatives
  • Respond in a reasonable timeframe — indefinite delay = bad faith
  • Provide written denial with explanation if accommodation is refused
  • Document the process — steward should request this documentation
  • Keep medical information confidential — separate from personnel file
  • Not retaliate for requesting accommodation or leave
Watch for

Common employer failures in ADA cases

  • Demanding full medical records rather than functional limitation documentation
  • Offering accommodation that doesn't address the actual limitation
  • "Undue hardship" claimed without analysis — requires specific evidence
  • Interactive process conducted only by email with no real discussion
  • Member placed on leave against their will instead of accommodated in place
  • HR and Work Connections giving conflicting guidance to the member
  • Accommodation tied to performance improvement or discipline timeline
  • Failure to reassign to vacant position as last resort when accommodation in current role not possible

Steward Procedure — When a Member Comes to You

Step-by-step
  1. Listen and triage. What is the member asking for — time off, workplace changes, RTW modifications, or all of the above? Which track(s) apply? Use the flowchart to confirm.
  2. Document the intake. Date, member name, condition (general — do not record diagnosis), what HR/Work Connections has said, what the member has already submitted. Log in dashboard.
  3. Establish timeline. Has a deadline been given? (Common: HR gives 5–10 business days to submit provider documentation.) Note it. Deadline pressure is an employer tactic — members don't have to rush.
  4. Advise on provider documentation. The note should describe functional limitations and recommended accommodation — NOT diagnosis. Member should review with their provider before submitting. See documentation guidance below.
  5. Assert representation rights. If any meeting is scheduled with HR or Work Connections, advise the member to request steward presence before the meeting. Weingarten rights apply in this context.
  6. Monitor the employer's response. HR has a response obligation. If they go silent, that's a steward issue. Follow up on the member's behalf.
  7. Assess for grievance. If accommodation is denied without process, leave is denied without basis, or discipline occurs in connection with leave or accommodation, evaluate grievance options under the CBA.
  8. Loop in Chief Steward for complex cases. Multi-track (FMLA + ADA + CBA), RTW disputes, denial of accommodation, or any case involving potential PWDCRA claim.

Key Legal Definitions

Disability (ADA)

A physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities. Includes conditions in remission or episodic (e.g., MS, lupus, depression). Post-ADAAA, courts construe this broadly.

Disability (PWDCRA)

A determinable physical or mental characteristic that substantially limits one or more major life activities OR is regarded as such by the employer. Slightly broader than ADA — does not require the same substantiality threshold in all cases.

Reasonable Accommodation

Any modification or adjustment that enables a qualified employee to perform essential job functions, without causing undue hardship. Not required to be the member's preferred accommodation — but must be effective.

Undue Hardship

Significant difficulty or expense. Michigan Medicine (U of M) as a large public employer faces a high bar to claim undue hardship. Must show specific evidence — not just "it's inconvenient" or "it sets a precedent."

Essential Job Functions

The fundamental duties of the position — not marginal tasks. Job descriptions matter here, but aren't dispositive. What does the employee actually spend most of their time doing? Steward should request current job description as part of any ADA case.

Interactive Process

An informal dialogue between employer and employee (and union when present) to identify effective accommodations. Both parties must participate in good faith. Employer cannot conduct the process unilaterally by email only.

Serious Health Condition (FMLA)

An illness, injury, impairment, or physical or mental condition requiring inpatient care OR continuing treatment by a health care provider. Covers most conditions requiring more than 3 consecutive days of incapacity plus ongoing treatment.
